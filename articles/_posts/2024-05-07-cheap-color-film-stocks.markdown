The fact that film, no matter the format or type, is getting more and more
expensive with seemingly no limit is certainly no secret to anyone involved in
analog photography. Especially as many film stocks are pretty much permanently
out of stock, sellers command even higher prices once they are available for
once. Gone are the days where one could find a 35mm roll of _Provia_ for just a
few bucks.

Some opt to shoot expired film to save cost, but that always brings a huge risk
with it. Your photos could turn out to have a nasty color cast, massive fogging,
be underexposed or even not develop at all in the worst case. So what if we want
to minimize the risk but still spend less? Enter low-end film.

### Technicalities and What to Expect

Low-end consumer films are typically very forgiving when it comes to _correct_
storage. They are designed to tolerate even the worst mishandling (although one
should still give good care as to how they store their film). Professional film
is designed to be shot right away and to be stored in a freezer if not, low-end
consumer film is expected to be shot quite a while after it was bought with
little effort spent on cold storage.

This consumer orientation also brings some downsides with it, though. Consumer
film is made to deliver results that are usable for someone without much of a
photographic background, no matter what situation they are in. This lack of
specialization means that better results can sometimes be achieved with a film
that is more specialized towards a certain scenario. Grain is often times more
pronounced in those films as well as most consumers do not really care about it
that much. The exposure latitude can also be a little narrower.

The data sheets of entry-level films are typically less detailed and generally
contain less information. This is of course to be expected but a bad thing for
the serious photographer. Professional films' data sheets typically include
concrete facts, numbers and graphs to work with.

### Kodak

A very popular option for cheap color negative film is _Kodak Gold 200_ and its
bigger sibling _Kodak UltraMax 400_. They are to be found at pretty much every
single drug store for 8-10€ a roll. Gold typically comes in rolls of 36 frames
while UltraMax is more commonly seen as 24 frame rolls. UltraMax costs a bit
more, typically about 1€, even though it has considerably less frames. Gold is
also commonly available in packs of three for about 20€.

Both films feature relatively warm colors, especially when compared to some
slide films like _Ektachrome_ or _Provia_. Their grain is far more pronounced
and often times quite visible when compared to professional color negative
films.

Both films are perfectly useable for most situations. Yet, I think the color and
contrast rendition is underwhelming in some scenarios. They are made for casual
shooting in all kinds of conditions, not for fine art, after all. If you are
typically shooting _Portra 160_ or 400, those two films might be something to
consider for you as they are somewhat similar in their rendering to Portra.

Another film stock even a little cheaper than Kodak Gold is _Kodak ColorPlus
200_. The difference between it and Gold is very faint. Some say that it is a
bit less warm and has a narrower exposure latitude, rendering it a bit more
contrasty as well. I can hardly make out a difference but I also did not do
side-by-side comparisons.

Really, the differences are negligible in my opinion. I have shot both and the
results looked very, very similar.
Some sources claim that ColorPlus is based on a 1970s emulsion whereas Gold is
newer. I say get whichever one is cheaper or try both for yourself.

### Fujifilm

Fujifilm used to offer a wide variety of budget film stocks but they
discontinued nearly all of them. Recently, even the popular _Superia X-Tra 400_,
which I shot a lot of when I first started with film, had to go. The only two
budget Fuji films remaining are the blandly named _Fujifilm 200_ and _Fujifilm
400_.

Kodak Gold and UltraMax films, especially because their packaging says "Made in
the USA" on the back. Whether they are Kodak films indeed is unknown to the
public. It is very likely though, especially when looking at scans on the
internet.

They do not really show up on drug store shelves, at least not around here where
I live. In fact, I have yet to come across them anywhere at all. If they were
available around here, I would have tested them.

### Slide Film

There are absolutely no low-end slide films around at the time of writing. There
used to be _Kodak Elitechrome_ and _Fujichrome Sensia_ among others but neither
are in production anymore. This is a shame as they yielded excellent results at
fairly low prices. Only professional-grade slide films are available these days
and they cost a lot.

E100_, which Fotoimpex currently sells at roughly 29€ a roll but still quite a
lot and not necessarily cheap. I have not tried re-spooled Ektachrome 100D thus
far but I would certainly like to in the future.

### Store Brand Films

Many years ago, drug store brands sometimes had their own film. Take _Schlecker_
as an example: they had films branded as _Schlecker Fotoland AS_. Those films
were typically re-badged Fujicolor films, as far as I can tell. Some even had
the green and red lines in the perforation area which are very typical for 35mm
Fujicolor films.

However, these films are a thing of the past. No store chain I know of still
carries their own film. Expired store brand film can often times still be found
online, typically stored horribly and sold at inflated prices. There is no real
reason to buy any of it.

### Beyond 35mm

Film larger than 35mm is entirely unavailable in drug stores. After all,
everything larger is considered a professional's format and professionals
typically get their film from photo stores. Still, that does not mean that there
are absolutely no budget color film stocks in larger formats, but the choice is
very, very limited:

Kodak Gold 200 is to be had in type 120 rolls. And that is about it. But then,
if you are shooting medium or even large format, cost is probably not your
number one priority.

### Is It Worth the Compromise?

It depends.

If absolutely all you care about is having your photos on film, certainly.
However, if you are like me and always go for a certain look in your shots,
absolutely not. All film stocks are made differently and therefore have
different characteristics built into them. If you have a concrete vision in
mind, do not try and force it onto budget films. Get the film that fits your
vision the best. Otherwise, you will be disappointed. I find this to be
especially true with color film.

I think such cheaper color films are great for learning and experimenting.
Whenever I want to try something entirely new, I opt for a roll of Kodak Gold
200 and if my idea does not work out as expected, it is not such a big deal.
Generally speaking, cheap color film is perfectly fine for quick snaps and
photos you do not care so much for or for work that is not so artistic.

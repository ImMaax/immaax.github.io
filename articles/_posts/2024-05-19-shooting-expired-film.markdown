Buying unexposed, expired film on the used market might be tempting: It is
cheaper than new film and allows you to test out discontinued film stocks. With
rising film prices, this topic is more relevant than ever before. While all that
sounds like a great concept, there are more things to consider than just the
price, though.

When I got more serious with my film photography, I started shooting expired
film simply for reasons of cost efficiency as it allowed me to shoot and learn
more. Since then, I have collected plenty of experience in the field.

### Storage Conditions and Age

Always ask sellers for how the film was stored. If the film was cold-stored, for
example in a freezer, it is likely still good and usable without issues.
However, if it was stored at room temperature in a closet somewhere, chances are
it has developed issues (see below for more). The latter is especially common
with 35mm consumer film as most consumers never even knew or cared about proper
film storage to begin with. Larger, professional formats like 120 or sheet film
tend to be cold-stored more often.

But beware: Just because a seller claims their film was cold-stored ever since
it was bought does not guarantee anything at all. As it appears, the definition
of "cold-stored" varies a lot between sellers. I have had film that was
supposedly always stored in the fridge and was not even that long expired turn
absolutely purple when developed.

Professional-grade film is especially prone to issues developing due to bad
storage conditions as it is typically designed to be shot shortly after being
bought. On the contrary side, consumer film stocks tend to last longer under bad
storage conditions for reasons stated above.

Furthermore, I recommend not buying material that is more than twenty years
expired. Unlike fine wine, film does not get better with age and your chances of
getting properly working film are higher with newer film. I once shot a 120 roll
of Ektachrome 64 that expired in 1985. Needless to say, there barely even were
images visible on the film once I had it developed. One roll of Agfa slide film
I was given once, which had expired roughly 23 years prior and was not stored
particularly well, had not just developed an incredibly strong blue color cast
but mold had formed on it as well. The images from this film were entirely
unusable.

If possible, buy multiple rolls from the same manufacturing batch. Shoot one
bracketed to get an idea of how to expose the others, which filtration is
needed and whether they are still worth shooting. If the seller has tested the
film recently prior to selling it, ask them for their experience with it or,
better even, some unedited scans. Do not pay the highly inflated prices some
sellers are trying to squeeze out of unknowing photographers. Only today, I saw
a 35mm canister of Velvia 50 whose expiry date and storage conditions were
unknown for a price of 26€. No matter how nice of a film stock Velvia 50 is,
this is an exceptionally bad offer. I recommend searching for offers by serious
photographers who know how film needs to be stored and whose stock is something
like five to ten years expired.

When being given old film by family and friends, expect the worst. I know how
that sounds but better be cautious than sorry. Use such film for experimenting
and testing only. Especially color film found in wardrobes and the like is
typically unusable: one roll of some old _Kodacolor_ stock I was given did not
produce any discernible images anymore, even at multiple stops overexposure.
Some other old _Kodak Gold_ and _Ultra Gold_ rolls I was given barely produced
an image and only did so with massive color shifts.

Unexposed color film has a tendency to develop color shifts when stored
incorrectly for too long, especially into the magenta or blue tones. Some
sellers and writers market this as a feature, claiming it allows for
experimental effects on photos. While that holds true, I think of it more of an
issue, at least when doing traditional photography.
Essentially, you lose all color predictability and end up with highly
unrealistic tones[^1]. I myself am not a fan of my pictures turning purple.
Depending on how strong the color shift is, it might be corrected digitally in
post.

What is more, film tends to lose sensitivity over time (i.e. the ISO number
drops below the specified box speed). I found this to be less of a problem with
E6 slide film. I typically shoot slide film at box speed, no matter how old it
is. However, C-41 negative film of unknown storage history typically requires
overexposure. I found overexposing one stop per decade (i.e. ISO 200 expired 20
years ago = ISO 50) to be a good general guideline. Again, test one roll from a
larger batch to be sure.

Black and white film tends to fare better, but it is not immune to aging. Of
course, you will not get color shifts. Lower speed b/w film can typically be
shot at box speed. Film of ISO 400 and higher tends to require some
overexposure, although not as much as comparable color negative film would.
In my experience, one stop per 15 years suffices. I have shot old _Agfapan 400_
and _Ilford HP5+_ from the mid 1990s of unknown storage conditions both at EI
100 instead of the box speed of 400 and the results came out great and properly
exposed.

Some _Kodak Tri-X Pan 400_ I recently acquired needed a little overexposure too.
I tried it at EI 320, but it could have used a little bit more, the negatives
still turned out somewhat thin, but usable. In the case of doubt, I would rather
over- than underexpose.

Of course, all this applies for film of unknown storage conditions. If you know
for a fact that your film was being refrigerated since it was bought, shoot it
at box speed, it will be fine.

### Kodachrome and Other Discontinued Process Films

Stay away from Kodachrome these days. There is not a single lab in the world
still developing it as the K14 chemicals required to process it are no longer
being made. In other words, while you can technically still shoot Kodachrome,
you cannot get it developed _properly_ anymore[^2]. That is a shame, I agree;
Kodachrome had beautiful color tones after all.

The same goes for other films designed for discontinued and obsolete processes,
such as [AP-41](http://analoguephotolab.com/agfa-process-41/).

Stick to E6, C-41 or regular black and white process films. If you have a lab
that develops Scala films, you could get those as well. Luckily, almost all
_younger_ expired film found on the market today uses one of the currently
supported processes, it is mostly ancient film that uses discontinued processes.
The processes named above have been the standard for decades now.

### Conclusion

Expired film carries huge risks. However, if you know what to look out for when
buying and are willing to take the risk of losing or corrupting your photos, go
ahead. I know, that sounds terrible. If you are scared by this (at least for
important work you should be) but still want to save some cash, feel free to
read
[my article about cheap color film stocks]({% post_url articles/2024-05-07-cheap-color-film-stocks %}).

Generally, I would advise to shop for expired film keeping all of the above in
mind and only using it for work that can easily be recreated. I like using
expired film occasionally for photo walks in my area. If the photos do not turn
out well, it is not such a huge deal.

No one said shooting film was affordable.

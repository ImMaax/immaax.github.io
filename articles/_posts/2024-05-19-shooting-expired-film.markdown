When I got more serious with my film photography, I started shooting expired
film simply for reasons of cost efficiency as it allowed me to shoot and learn
more than if I were using fresh film.

### Storage

Always ask sellers for how the film was stored. If the film was cold-stored, for
example in a freezer, it is likely still good and usable without issues.
However, if it was stored at room temperature in a closet somewhere, chances are
it has developed issues (see below for more). The latter is especially common
with 35mm consumer film as most consumers never even knew or cared about proper
film storage to begin with. Larger, professional formats and film stocks like
Velvia or Provia in 120 or sheet film tend to be cold-stored more often.

### Issues

Unlike fine wine, film does not get better with age. Therefore, be extra
cautious with stock that is more than twenty years expired. As unused
photographic materials age, the likelihood of issues developing (or getting
worse) rises. Some of the most common issues include

- a loss of sensitivity and contrast,
- a visible increase in graininess,
- color shifts,
- high levels of fogging, possibly uneven,
- exposures already made on the film,
- moisture on the film emulsion (damaging said emulsion or causing fungus to
  build up) and
- dried up, brittle film base or glue (the latter being applicable to 35mm and
  roll film)

I have encountered all of the above. Some film was not salvageable anymore.
However, some less extreme color shifts can be corrected digitally:

As film tends to lose sensitivity to light over time, the ISO number drops below
the original ISO the film once had when it came from the factory. I found this
to be less of a problem with E6 slide film. I typically shoot slide film at box
speed, no matter how old it is. However, C-41 negative film of unknown storage
history typically requires overexposure. I found overexposing one stop per
decade of expiration (i.e. ISO 200 expired 20 years ago = ISO 50 today) to be a
good general guideline.

Black and white film tends to fare better, but it is not immune to aging. Of
course, you will not get color shifts. Lower speed b/w film typically retains
its original speed. Film of ISO 400 and higher tends to require some
overexposure, although not as much as comparable color negative film would.
In my experience, one stop per 15 years suffices. I have shot old _Agfapan 400_
and _Ilford HP5+_ from the mid 1990s of unknown storage conditions both at EI
100 instead of the box speed of 400 and the results came out great and properly
exposed, matching my metering.

Some _Kodak Tri-X Pan 400_ I recently acquired needed a little overexposure too.
I stubbornly tried it at EI 320 to see what would be possible, but it could have
used a lot more, the negatives turned out somewhat thin and clearly did not
reflect my metering, but they were usable enough for scanning purposes. In the
case of doubt, I would rather over- than underexpose.

Yet, keep in mind that you should determine a personal film speed by running
tests against your process (or lab; more on that below) as what I am providing
here is just a rough "averaged" guideline from my experience! Cold-stored film
should not exhibit these issues to such a high degree, as stated in the
beginning.

### Testing

Buy multiple rolls (or sheet film boxes) from the same manufacturing batch.
Shoot one roll (or some sheets) for testing purposes as to know not only what to
expect from the rest of the bulk (is the film still useable?) but also how to
expose and process it. For learning about this procedure, I recommend reading
*Zone VI Workshop: The Fine Print In Black & White Photography* by Fred Picker.
In it, he gives good and precise advice on the topic. Reading the book in its
entirety is absolutely worthwhile, by the way.

If the seller has tested the film prior to selling it, ask them for their
experience with it or, better even, some unedited scans. Their statements
regarding the film's condition should only serve to give you a rough first idea
and as help with the buying decision but not as a guarantee.

When being given old film by family and friends, expect the worst.
Most people stored their film in some cupboard or closet at room temperature and
under no further protection from the environment and as most people never kept
big lots of film of the same emulsion number around, extensive testing on such
film might not be possible, leaving a lot of variables unknown and the results
unpredictable.
I have had good luck with old black and white film I was given by a friend, but
almost none of the color film he gave me turned out at all with some rolls not
even producing discernible images anymore.

Not doing any tests is unnecessarily dangerous. It would be rather likely that
you end up with entirely dissatisfactory results. Running a proper testing
routine not only costs part of the film you have just bought but also lots of
precious time. If you think that is unacceptable (which is totally fair!), I
would recommend using fresh film exclusively.

### Kodachrome and Other Discontinued Process Films

Stay away from Kodachrome these days. There is not a single lab in the world
still developing it as the *K14* chemicals required to process it are no longer
being made. In other words, while you can technically still shoot Kodachrome,
you cannot get it developed _properly_ anymore[^1]. That is a shame, I agree;
Kodachrome had beautiful color tones after all.

The same goes for other films designed for discontinued and obsolete processes,
such as [AP-41](http://analoguephotolab.com/agfa-process-41/).
Such film can often times be developed in regular black and white chemistry. In
case you decide to go down that road, expect the worst.

Stick to E6, C-41 or regular black and white process films. Luckily, almost all
_younger_ expired film found on the market today uses one of the currently
supported processes, it is mostly ancient film that uses discontinued processes.
The processes named above have been the standard for decades now.

### Conclusion

Expired film carries huge risks if used without proper testing. However, if you
know what to look out for when buying in order to mitigate the risk of total
failure and are willing to do extensive testing, it can offer a much cheaper,
more labor-intensive alternative to fresh stock.

The real key to success is testing and experimenting here.
